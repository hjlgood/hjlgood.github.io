A cybersecurity and IT-management software provider primarily serving managed service providers, or MSPs.

At **$4.23 per share**, N-able’s market capitalization is approximately **$793 million**. Yet management expects roughly **$556.5 million of 2026 revenue** and about **$169 million of adjusted EBITDA**.

That disconnect makes NABL look unusually cheap. The catch is equally clear: **heavy debt, weak GAAP earnings, slowing near-term organic growth, and persistent stock-based compensation**. ([N-able Investors][1])

## Competitive Moat

N-able’s moat is **workflow integration rather than technological monopoly**.

An MSP may use N-able to monitor endpoints, deploy patches, manage passwords, back up data, respond to security threats and operate its service desk. Replacing that infrastructure can require retraining employees, migrating customer devices, rewriting scripts and risking service disruption.

Additional advantages include:

* **Telemetry from more than 11 million endpoints**, supporting automation and threat detection.
* A distribution channel of thousands of MSPs, each of which serves multiple end customers.
* Mission-critical backup and security workloads with low tolerance for downtime.
* Cross-selling opportunities across RMM, backup, EDR, MDR and cloud management.
* Nearly entirely recurring subscription revenue.

The moat is real but not unassailable. MSPs commonly use multiple vendors, and competitors such as Kaseya and ConnectWise can bundle products aggressively. ([N-able Investors][5])

## Bull Scenario

The bull case is straightforward: **NABL is priced as though growth is disappearing, while the business is still expanding ARR at high-single-digit rates and generating a 30% adjusted EBITDA margin.**

At the current price:

* Forward EV/revenue is approximately **1.9×**.
* Forward EV/adjusted EBITDA is only about **6.3×**.
* The stock trades around **book value**.
* Subscription revenue is nearly 99% of total revenue.
* Management expects meaningful improvement in unlevered free cash flow.

Suppose N-able sustains **8% revenue growth**, integrates Adlumin, reduces debt and receives an **8×–9× EBITDA multiple**. At $169 million of EBITDA, enterprise value could reach roughly **$1.35 billion–$1.52 billion**.

After subtracting current net debt, equity value would be approximately **$5.70–$6.60 per share**, before accounting for future debt reduction. A stronger rerating toward 10× EBITDA could support a price above **$7.50**.

## Bear Scenario: Red-Team View

The bullish argument relies heavily on adjusted EBITDA—and that is precisely where investors should attack it.

N-able incurred **$11.1 million of stock-based compensation in Q1**, equivalent to more than **8% of revenue**. Annualized, that is roughly $44 million of compensation that adjusted EBITDA largely ignores.

The company also has:

* Approximately **$393 million of debt**.
* More than **$7 million of quarterly net interest expense**.
* Negative trailing GAAP earnings.
* Q2 constant-currency growth guidance of just **4%**.
* Customer concentration in an intensely competitive MSP channel.
* Acquisition-related integration and cross-selling assumptions.
* Share count growth from equity compensation.

If organic growth slips toward 3%–5%, investors may stop valuing NABL as growth software and treat it as a leveraged mature IT vendor. At **5×–6× adjusted EBITDA**, the equity could be worth roughly **$3.00–$4.00 per share**, particularly if free cash flow disappoints.

The unfortunate reality is that **a low EV/EBITDA multiple is not automatically cheap when EBITDA excludes recurring compensation expenses**.

### DCF interpretation

A conservative DCF should not simply annualize Q1 cash flow because working capital varies materially quarter to quarter.

Using normalized free cash flow of approximately **$70 million**, mid-single-digit long-term growth and a double-digit discount rate produces an equity value near **$5.30 per share**.

A more optimistic scenario—with free cash flow approaching $90 million as margins improve and debt falls—supports **$6.50–$7.00**. A stagnation scenario falls below $4.

## Bottom Line

N-able combines characteristics that rarely appear together: **nearly 99% subscription revenue, high gross margins, a 30% adjusted EBITDA target—and a distressed single-digit EBITDA multiple**.

The stock could rerate sharply if management proves that organic ARR growth can remain above 8%, converts EBITDA into cash and uses that cash to reduce debt.

But the market’s skepticism is rational. **GAAP profitability is thin, stock compensation is high, interest expense is material, and Q2 organic growth is expected to slow to approximately 4%.**

My base-case fair value is **$4.75–$5.75**, with a bull-case range of **$6.50–$7.50** and a bear-case range of **$3.00–$4.00**. The risk/reward is favorable for investors comfortable with small-cap software execution risk, but NABL has not yet earned the status of a high-quality compounder.

**The question for investors is this: should N-able be valued on its attractive adjusted EBITDA—or on the far smaller amount of earnings left after stock compensation, interest and acquisition costs?**
